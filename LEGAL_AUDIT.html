Two additional problems surfaced during verification: the privacy policy does not describe what the app actually collects (Sentry crash data tied to user IDs; Microsoft Clarity on the web site), and the app offers account creation with no in-app account deletion, which violates Apple's App Store Review Guideline 5.1.1(v) outright.

1. Age gate — none exists

There is no age verification of any kind. Signup (app/(auth)/login.tsx) collects email + password (or Google/Apple sign-in); onboarding collects a name, motivations, and a daily goal. An exhaustive search for age, birthday, DOB, minor, COPPA, and parental-consent strings across the app, database schema (db.sql), and migrations found zero hits. The privacy policy is silent on children.

Why it matters: the app collects PII (email, name) from anyone who signs up. With no age screen and no children's-data language, a US user under 13 puts you in COPPA territory (FTC penalties are per-violation), and EU/UK users under 13–16 raise GDPR Article 8 consent issues. A language-learning app is plausibly attractive to minors, which weakens a "not directed at children" defence.

Evidence No age fields in db.sql:4–19 (users table) or services/api/users.ts:6–22 (UserRow). No age-rating or parental configuration in app.json. Privacy policy (web/privacy.html) never mentions minors.

The bigger problem: the mobile app links to nothing

  • No privacy-policy or terms link anywhere in the app — not at signup, not in settings, no legal screen. The only reference is the web-site footer (web/site-app.jsx:324), which links the privacy page only.
  • No consent language at signup. A design mockup (spec_docs · pl-auth.jsx:64) shows "By continuing you agree to our Terms & Privacy" — it was never built.
  • No Terms of Service document exists anywhere in the project.
  • The privacy policy that does exist covers only the waitlist email. It says nothing about account data, learning-progress data, Sentry crash reports (which attach the user ID — app/_layout.tsx:223), or the Microsoft Clarity tracker embedded in the policy page itself (web/privacy.html:11–17). An inaccurate policy is arguably worse than none — it's an FTC "deceptive practices" angle and it contradicts App Store privacy-label declarations.
  • Apple guideline 5.1.1(i) requires a privacy-policy link inside the app; 5.1.1(v) requires in-app account deletion for apps with account creation. Neither exists (deletion is offered only "by emailing us" in the web policy).

3. User-generated content — claim refuted

Good news: this attack fails. The app has no user-to-user surface. No chat, comments, posts, shared profiles, or leaderboards. name/avatar_url exist in the schema but render only on the user's own profile (app/(tabs)/profile.tsx:55); avatars aren't used in any UI. The feedback form (services/api/feedback.ts) is private — RLS restricts each row to its author. So Apple's UGC rules (guideline 1.2: filtering, reporting, blocking) don't currently apply, and there's nothing to moderate.

Caveat: the waitlist form has a checkbox opting users into a WhatsApp/Instagram community (web/site-questionnaire.jsx:264–265). Those are external, unmoderated-by-the-app spaces; nothing about them appears in the privacy policy. If you actively run those groups, member conduct there is your problem reputationally even if not in-app. Disclose the opt-in's consequences in the policy.

4. Marketing email — collection fine, promises inconsistent

Emails are collected via the web waitlist form into the Supabase waitlist table (db.sql:175–191). No marketing email has ever been sent — there's no email-service integration at all — so there is no CAN-SPAM violation today. But the paperwork is contradictory and the machinery for compliant sending doesn't exist:

  • Scope mismatch: the form promises "One email when we launch. That's the whole commitment." (site-questionnaire.jsx:201), while the privacy policy says the email is used to "notify you at launch and send occasional updates" (privacy.html:172). Consent obtained under the narrower promise doesn't cover "occasional updates" — sending more than the one launch email would breach the stated basis for collection (a GDPR/consumer-protection problem for EU/UK recipients, and simply a broken promise for the FTC).
  • Unsubscribe is manual only: "email kannadabeku@gmail.com" (privacy.html:198–200). CAN-SPAM technically permits a reply-address opt-out, but any future email must carry a functioning opt-out mechanism, a physical postal address, and honor opt-outs within 10 business days — none of which is set up. There's no suppression list, no unsubscribed flag in the schema.
  • Done right: the community opt-in checkbox defaults to unchecked (community_optin DEFAULT false, db.sql:189) — affirmative consent, correctly separated from the waitlist itself.
  • Side finding: the web form posts to Supabase with the anon key hardcoded in client JS (site-questionnaire.jsx:12–13) and an anonymous-insert RLS policy. That's the normal Supabase pattern, but confirm the waitlist table has no anonymous select policy — otherwise the entire email list is publicly readable, which would be a reportable breach.

5. Liability disclaimers — none anywhere

  • No Terms of Service means no limitation of liability, no "as-is" warranty disclaimer, no governing-law clause, no arbitration/class-action provisions. Every user relationship is governed by default consumer law of wherever the user happens to live.
  • Emergency phrases with no disclaimer: app/emergency.tsx presents "Please help", "Police", "Accident", "Danger", "Stop" phrases styled as an all-red urgency surface, with a "SAFETY ISSUE" badge, advertised as "Works offline". Nothing tells the user these are educational practice phrases, that pronunciation via device TTS isn't guaranteed, or that the feature is not a substitute for calling emergency services. Notably, an [Unverified] content warning that used to render here was deliberately removed (spec_docs/Sameecha/spec_content_integrity.md:55–58) — a plaintiff would enjoy that document. This is the single most concrete "someone got hurt relying on your app" fact pattern in the product.
  • One claim that checks out: "Recordings stay on your device" (app.json:19, PermissionDialog.tsx:29–35) is accurate — the recording code (services/audio/deviceTtsAudioService.ts:118–143) keeps audio local and discards it. Keep it that way; that sentence is now a warranty.

Prioritized fixes

  1. Write a Terms of Service (limitation of liability, as-is disclaimer, minimum age 13+, governing law) and publish it at kannadabeku.com/terms. This single document addresses claims 1, 2, and 5 at once.
  2. Rewrite the privacy policy to cover the app, not just the waitlist: account data, progress data, Sentry crash reporting (with user IDs), Microsoft Clarity on the web, the WhatsApp/Instagram community opt-in, and a children's-data section.
  3. Add legal links + consent line in the app: "By continuing you agree to our Terms & Privacy Policy" on the auth screen (the mockup already exists), plus links in settings.
  4. Add in-app account deletion — an Apple review requirement (5.1.1(v)) independent of any lawsuit.
  5. Add an age attestation at signup (13+ self-declaration is the minimal defensible floor) and state the minimum age in the ToS.
  6. Add a one-line disclaimer to the Emergency screen ("Practice phrases — in a real emergency, call 112").
  7. Align the email promises — either the form says "occasional updates" too, or the policy says "one launch email"; add an unsubscribed flag before any send.
  8. Fix the web infrastructure: add a DNS record (or redirect) for www.kannadabeku.com, and make unknown paths like /terms.html return a real 404 instead of the homepage.
  9. Verify the waitlist table has no anonymous select policy in the Supabase dashboard.
