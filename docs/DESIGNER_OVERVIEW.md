# Kannada Beku — A Designer's Guide to the App

Welcome! This is a friendly tour of the app, written so you can understand the whole
thing without reading a line of code. By the end you'll know what the app does, how a
learner moves through it, and the look and feel you'll be designing within.

---

## The big picture

Kannada Beku teaches people how to *speak* everyday Kannada — the language of Bengaluru
and Karnataka. It's aimed at adults who've moved to the city (or are about to) and want
to hold real conversations fast, without slogging through grammar textbooks. It's a
phone app, for both iPhone and Android.

Three ideas drive everything:

- **Speak from day one.** Learners practise real phrases, not drills.
- **Never get stuck.** There's an offline "emergency" section with survival phrases for
  autos, shops, and the street — usable even with no signal and no login.
- **Five minutes a day.** The app rewards small, consistent habits with visible streaks.

The lessons go from L1 to L8, each broken into a few bite-sized parts. Before Lesson 1
there's a short **Beginners Guide** that teaches the sounds and the alphabet. And once
a learner has finished something, four little **games** let them play with what they've
learned.

A quick note on how words are shown throughout the app (and this doc): every Kannada
word appears three ways — the **script** (ನಮಸ್ಕಾರ), the **transliteration** (*namaskāra*,
how you'd say it), and the **English meaning**. That three-layer pattern is everywhere.

## How a learner gets around

When someone opens the app, they pass through a brand splash screen, log in if needed,
and — if it's their first time — go through a short setup (more on that below). After
that, they land in the main app, which is organised around a **bottom bar with four
tabs**:

- **Home** — the daily check-in. Where you've left off, today's goals, your streak.
- **Learn** — the curriculum: the eight lessons plus the Beginners Guide.
- **Games** — the four games to practise with.
- **Profile** — your stats, your goal, and settings.

On top of those tabs, full-screen experiences open as needed: the first-time **setup**,
the **lesson player**, each **game**, the **emergency phrases**, the **Beginners Guide**,
and **settings**.

## The four main screens

### Home

This is the screen a returning learner sees most. At a glance it tells them how they're
doing and nudges them back in. Near the top is their **streak** — a little flame with a
day count. Below that, a warm greeting by name, then the heart of the screen: a card
with **three progress rings** for the day — one each for Listening, Speaking, and
Practising — that fill up as they go.

Underneath, a **"continue where you left off"** card drops them straight back into their
next lesson, and a **words-learned banner** celebrates how much vocabulary they've
picked up. There's also a quick shortcut to the emergency phrases for moments when
they're out in the world and stuck.

### Learn

This is the curriculum laid out as a journey. At the top sits the **Beginners Guide**
(the sounds-and-alphabet primer they can revisit any time). Below it, the eight lessons
appear as a vertical path. Each lesson shows its state at a glance:

- **Done** lessons get a gold tile and a checkmark.
- The **active** lesson is highlighted in red with a play button, and if it has several
  parts, a little segmented bar shows how far through they are.
- **Locked** lessons are faded with a lock; tapping one gently explains why it's not
  open yet.

The eight lessons, in order:

1. **Greetings** — hello, and how are you
2. **Names** — I, you, "my name is"
3. **Wanting** — I want, I don't want
4. **Pointing** — this, that, here, there
5. **Easy verbs** — come, eat, laugh
6. **Questions** — who, what, where
7. **Hard verbs** — see, do, play
8. **Putting it together** — combining everything

### Games

A playground of four games. They stay **locked until the learner finishes their first
lesson part** — there's no point quizzing someone on words they haven't met. Locked
games look dimmed, and a friendly note reminds everyone that the games only ever use
phrases they've already learned.

### Profile

The "how am I doing" screen. It shows their name and when they started, a big **overall
progress** figure that blends lessons and games, and two stat cards for lessons
completed and words learned. It also surfaces the **goal** they set during setup
(their learning style, minutes per day, and reasons), and gives them access to settings:
reminders, audio and pronunciation, and help. Sign-out lives here too.

## The four games

Every game only uses vocabulary the learner has already seen, and they all end on the
same tidy results screen with a score and a "play again or move on" choice.

- **Quick Quiz** — a fast multiple-choice round against a timer. See a Kannada word,
  tap the right English meaning before the clock runs out.
- **Dictation** — hear a phrase, then *build* it in Kannada by tapping syllable tiles
  together. It accepts a few reasonable spellings, so learners aren't punished for
  small variations.
- **Opposites** — see a word and choose its opposite from four options.
- **Conversations** — a little roleplay. A character speaks, and the learner picks a
  Kannada reply; the conversation unfolds turn by turn.

There's also the **Emergency phrases** screen — not a game, but an important safety net.
It's a list of survival phrases sorted by how urgent they are and grouped by situation
(auto, shop, street). Each phrase shows what to *say*, what to *show* (the script), what
it means, and a button to hear it out loud. It works completely offline and needs no
login — designed for the moment someone is actually stuck on the street.

## What a lesson feels like

A lesson isn't a wall of text — it's a guided sequence that eases the learner in and
builds up:

1. **Set the scene** — here's the situation you'll use this in.
2. **Meet the words** — a small set of new vocabulary.
3. **Practise the words** — listen and say them back.
4. **Meet the phrases** — full sentences, with a note on how they work.
5. **Practise the phrases** — try the sentences.
6. **Recap** — a quick review of everything.
7. **Real world** — a little challenge to use it for real.
8. **Done** — a celebration and a clear next step.

Longer lessons are split into parts, so a learner can do just one part in a spare five
minutes. Finishing any part keeps their streak alive and helps unlock the games.

## How progress is shown

Four ideas tell the learner they're getting somewhere, and the design leans on all of
them:

- **Streak** — how many days in a row they've shown up. The little flame.
- **Daily rings** — separate rings for Listening, Speaking, and Practising, each with
  its own daily target, reset every night.
- **Words learned** — a growing count of the vocabulary they've collected.
- **Overall progress** — one headline percentage on the Profile, blending lessons
  completed with how well they're doing in the games.

## The look and feel

The app has a mature, well-established visual style with a name: **"Chunky Kit."** It's
deliberate and consistent, and most of it is considered settled — so think of this as
the world you're designing *within*, rather than a blank page.

**The personality.** Bold, rounded, and tactile. The signature move is a "lip" — buttons
and cards have a solid colored bottom edge, and when you press one, the top face
physically drops down onto the lip, like pressing a real, chunky key. It feels playful
and satisfying rather than flat and digital.

**The colors.** The palette comes straight from Karnataka's identity: a deep **Mysore
red** and a warm **turmeric gold**, sitting on soft, warm, sandstone-cream backgrounds.
Red is for action and importance; gold is for reward and encouragement; a burnt orange
signals "locked" or "offline." Text is always a warm near-black, never a cold pure
black or grey. And there's a firm rule: **no blues, greens, teals, or corals** — the
warmth is part of the brand.

**Separation without lines.** Instead of drawing borders between things, the design
shifts the background tone slightly. There's really only one faint hairline in the whole
app, and it's barely there on purpose — "felt, not seen."

**The type.** Three typefaces, each with a clear job: a rounded, friendly face (**Baloo**)
for headings, big numbers, and buttons; a clean sans (**DM Sans**) for body text and
the transliterations; and a dedicated Kannada face (**Noto Sans Kannada**) for the
script. Headings are big and tight; body text is calm and readable.

**Motion.** Small, springy touches — the press-and-drop of a button, rings sweeping as
they fill, the streak flame giving a little wiggle, an audio button pulsing as it plays.
All of it politely disappears if the learner has "reduce motion" turned on.

There's a healthy kit of ready-made pieces already built — the chunky buttons, the
floating tab bar, progress rings, the streak pill, audio buttons, and a full set of
"something's loading / something went wrong / you're offline / nothing here yet" screens.
Wherever possible, reach for those rather than building new ones.

## Want to go deeper?

The detailed, authoritative design spec lives in **docs/foundation/DESIGN.md**, with a
companion spec for modals and overlays in **docs/foundation/MODALS.md**. The actual
color, type, and spacing values live in the project's `constants` folder, and the
reusable building blocks are in `components/ui` and `components/states`.

One thing worth knowing before you change anything: most of the design is intentionally
**locked**, and the colors and styles are shared across the whole app — so a single
tweak ripples everywhere. That's by design, but it means it's always worth a quick
check-in with the team before reworking a core token or pattern.
